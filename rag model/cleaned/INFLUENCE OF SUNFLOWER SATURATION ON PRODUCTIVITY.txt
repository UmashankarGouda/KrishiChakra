### KEY FINDINGS  
- **Yield improvements**:  
  - Highest sunflower yield (2.82 t/ha) achieved in a seven-field crop rotation with 14.3% sunflower saturation.  
  - Yield declines as sunflower saturation increases: 20% (2.67 t/ha), 25% (2.67 t/ha), 33.3% (sharp decline), 50% (significant reduction).  
  - Winter wheat yield stable (4.59–4.65 t/ha) across rotations with 14.3–33.3% sunflower.  
  - Pea yield ranges: 1.76–1.89 t/ha.  
- **Soil health benefits**:  
  - Rational crop rotations improve soil fertility, nutrient availability, and biological activity.  
  - Crop residues and tillage systems (traditional, mini-till, strip-till) contribute to soil restoration.  
- **Disease reduction**:  
  - Proper rotation reduces weed density, pests, and diseases, lowering control costs.  
- **Economic impact**:  
  - Sunflower profitability drives increased cultivation, but excessive saturation reduces overall crop rotation productivity.

### CONCLUSIONS  
- **Main recommendations for farmers**:  
  - Limit sunflower saturation to ≤20% in crop rotations to maintain yield and soil health.  
  - Prioritize diverse rotations with grains (wheat, barley), legumes (pea), and row crops (corn).  
  - Avoid returning sunflower to the same field more frequently than every 8–10 years.  
- **Best practices for crop rotation**:  
  - Combine crops with high residue returns (e.g., corn) to enhance soil fertility.  
  - Use tillage systems (strip-till, mini-till) to optimize residue management.  
  - Replace part of sunflower area with corn or rapeseed to balance profitability and sustainability.
